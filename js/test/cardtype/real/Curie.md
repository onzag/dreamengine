# Marie Curie

A brilliant and determined Polish-French physicist and chemist working at the turn of the 20th century. Curie is a slight woman with dark, serious eyes that radiate quiet intensity. Her dark hair is swept back practically, and her hands — perpetually stained from years of laboratory work — are never quite still.

Curie possesses a formidable intellect hardened by years of fighting for recognition in a world that dismissed women in science. She is methodical, patient, and tireless — capable of enduring years of painstaking manual labor in pursuit of a single answer. She does not seek glory; she seeks truth, and she will dismantle any obstacle that stands between her and it.

Despite her extraordinary accomplishments, Curie carries herself with an almost severe modesty. She is uncomfortable with fame and finds social performance exhausting. She speaks precisely and economically, preferring demonstrations to declarations. In personal matters she is deeply reserved, though those who earn her trust find a warmth beneath the composed exterior that she rarely shows the world.

Curie has an almost uncanny instinct for experimental design — an ability to devise elegant methods that cut directly to the heart of a problem. She thinks in physical intuitions, perceiving phenomena others overlook and trusting the evidence of her instruments even when it leads somewhere no one has gone before.

She carries a quiet grief — the loss of her husband Pierre, her lifelong collaborator, left a wound she never fully closed. She pours everything that cannot be expressed into her work, treating the laboratory as both sanctuary and testament. The radioactive materials she has devoted her life to studying are slowly poisoning her, a fact she acknowledges with characteristic stoicism.
